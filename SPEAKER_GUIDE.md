## PRESENTATION BREAKDOWN WITH TIMING & DELIVERY NOTES

### SEGMENT 1: OPENING [0:00-0:30]
**Tone:** Warm, direct, conversational

```
"Good morning, judges. I'm [Your Name], and I want to ask you a question:

How many subscriptions do you have right now?

Netflix. Spotify. Cloud storage. Adobe. Slack. Gym membership.

Now, how much are you paying for subscriptions you forgot about?"
```

**Delivery Notes:**
- Pause after "How many subscriptions do you have right now?"
- Make it feel like a genuine question, not rhetorical
- The list should feel personal to judges (they recognize themselves)
- Slight pause before the final question to build anticipation

**Judge Psychology:** 
- Opens with question → judges engage (not just listening, but thinking)
- Relatable scenario → judges feel the problem personally
- You're not selling yet → you're connecting

### SEGMENT 2: THE PROBLEM [0:30-1:30]
**Tone:** Empathetic, concerned, but not doom-and-gloom — hopeful

```
"Most people can't answer that question. They can't name them all. 
And that's exactly why companies love subscriptions. 
They're designed to be forgotten. 

Hidden recurring charges. Silent trial-to-paid conversions. 
And by the time you notice, weeks of money have already gone through.

This is the problem we're solving today.

'Money You Don't Remember Spending.'

Here's what happens:

Week 1: You sign up for Netflix free trial. Excited. Ready to binge.

Week 2: You watch a few episodes. Life gets busy.

Week 3: You see a charge on your card. $14.99. You forgot the trial ended.

Week 4: Another $14.99. And another. Now you're $60 into something 
you haven't used in weeks.

This repeats across your entire digital life:
- Hidden renewals without warning
- Forgotten subscriptions you stopped using months ago
- Unexpected charges after trials end
- Difficult cancellation processes designed to trap you
- Merchants counting on you giving up

The result? The average person loses $200-400 per year to subscriptions 
they don't use or don't remember.

That's not a small problem. That's systemic."
```

**Delivery Notes:**
- Week 1-4 sequence: One per breath. Slow. Let it sink in.
- "$14.99" should sound almost ridiculous (emphasize the accumulation)
- Bullet points: Say each one with equal weight, but the last one slightly frustrated ("counting on you giving up")
- "$200-400" — emphasize this number. Let it hang.
- Final sentence: Slow, serious, strong.

**Judge Psychology:**
- Personal narrative builds empathy
- Numbers prove it's not just anecdotal
- Systemic framing = bigger problem = bigger opportunity
- Judges think: "I've experienced this. It IS frustrating."

**Tone:** Confident, clear, excited but controlled

```

Here's what we created:

1. SUBSCRIPTIONS DASHBOARD
Every subscription in one place. You can see:
- What you're paying (the exact amount and frequency)
- When it renews (the next charge date highlighted)
- Risk level (visual alerts for high-risk subscriptions)
- Cancellation status (Active, Cancelling, or Cancelled)

[PAUSE — let them visualize]

2. RENEWAL CALENDAR
A timeline view of all upcoming charges. 
Never be surprised by a renewal again. 
You see exactly when money is leaving your account — days in advance.

[PAUSE]

3. DISPUTE STUDIO — OUR INNOVATION
This is where we solve the biggest pain point: fighting back with proof.

When you get an unexpected charge, our Dispute Studio automatically builds your case.

It does something no competitor has:
It shows a chronological timeline of exactly what happened:
- Event 1: Free trial started
- Event 2: Trial was supposed to end April 8
- Event 3: Charge hit April 9 (1 day early)
- Event 4: User attempted cancellation
- Event 5: Cancellation was never processed
```

**Delivery Notes:**
- Dashboard section: Fast, energetic. Four points. Check them off mentally as you say them.
- "Under 5 seconds" — emphasize. This is a design principle, not a throwaway claim.
- Pause after "Here's what we created:" and "DISPUTE STUDIO — OUR INNOVATION" for emphasis
- Timeline section: Say each event on a new mental breath. Slow. Clear. The "1 day early" should feel like a gotcha moment.
- "No competitor has this" — say with quiet confidence
- Evidence section: Same rhythm as timeline. Each piece stacks to make the case stronger.
- Final sentences: Slow, clear, strong. "A case a bank can actually act on." is the payoff.

**Judge Psychology:**
- Three features = complete vision (not scattered ideas)
- Timeline explanation = judges understand the "aha" moment
- Evidence section = judges see it's thoughtful and comprehensive
- Emphasis on "no competitor" = they recognize the innovation
- "Bank can actually act on" = judges think about the business viability

## JUDGE ENGAGEMENT STRATEGIES

### During Presentation:

**Strategy 1: Direct Eye Contact**
- When you open, make eye contact with one judge, hold 3 seconds
- When you say a key point, look at a different judge
- During closing, sweep eye contact across all judges
- DO NOT read from slides

**Strategy 2: Pause for Effect**
- After key questions, pause 2-3 seconds
- After revealing a problem, pause 1-2 seconds
- Judges will use this time to nod, think, engage
- Silence builds anticipation

**Strategy 3: Use Judges' Assumptions**
- "You probably have Netflix" — they do, so they relate
- "You know how frustrating this is" — judges have felt this too
- "You're skeptical about tech companies" — judges are, so you're aligned

**Strategy 4: The "Aha" Moment**
- Timeline of events shows "charged 1 day early" — this is the gotcha
- Evidence section shows no renewal warning email — this is the proof
- One-click export/email — this is the power
- Build toward these moments

**Strategy 5: Numbers That Stick**
- Not: "Many people lose money"
- But: "330M people, $200-400 each, $87B total"
- Numbers make it real, not just a nice story

## HANDLING JUDGE QUESTIONS

**If judges ask: "How do you make money?"**

*Answer:* "Freemium model. Dashboard is free so users get immediate value. Dispute resolution is premium ($4.99/month). We also see enterprise B2B opportunity with credit card companies and banks. Each charge reversal saves banks money on manual dispute handling."

**If judges ask: "What about privacy/security?"**

*Answer:* "Session-based security with zero token exposure to frontend. Each user's data is isolated. We follow HTTPS encryption and WCAG AA accessibility standards. For demo purposes, we use PIN authentication, but production would integrate with OAuth."

**If judges ask: "Who is your competition?"**

*Answer:* "Competitors focus on expense tracking (Expensify, Bill Shark). We're unique because we focus specifically on subscriptions AND include dispute resolution with evidence compilation. No competitor has the Dispute Studio feature."

**If judges ask: "What's your go-to-market strategy?"**

*Answer:* "Consumer-first: Free dashboard drives activation. B2B second: Partnership with Visa, Mastercard, bank apps. We're aiming for 10K active users in month one through organic word-of-mouth."

**Judges are asking:**

1. **Does this problem exist?** (Yes, $87B proof)
2. **Is the solution innovative?** (Yes, Dispute Studio is unique)
3. **Can the team execute?** (Yes, they built a production system in 5 days)
4. **Is there a market?** (Yes, 330M potential customers)
5. **Will users adopt it?** (Yes, because it saves them money)
6. **Can we invest in this?** (Yes, clear financial model, real impact)

## FINAL PRACTICE TIPS

1. **Read the script OUT LOUD 3 times**
   - First read: Pronunciation, flow
   - Second read: Timing (should be 4:45-5:00)
   - Third read: Delivery, emotion

2. **Time yourself**
   - Write timestamps in margins
   - Practice hitting "1:30 mark for problem" checkpoint
   - If you're under 4:45, you're too fast; if over 5:00, too slow

3. **Record yourself**
   - Play it back
   - Notice: Do you sound passionate or monotone?
   - Are you pausing for effect?
   - Do you make eye contact (with phone camera if practicing)?

4. **Anticipate questions**
   - Have 3 questions prepared
   - Practice answers (30 seconds max)
   - End each answer with a statement, not a question

5. **Visualize success**
   - Imagine judges nodding during Dispute Studio explanation
   - Imagine them leaning forward at the market size reveal
   - Imagine them saying "Let's invest" at the end
   - Confidence comes from preparation
